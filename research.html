Research

My research focuses on understanding how plants respond to global environmental change e.g. increasing CO2, temperature and changing water availability. My work is at the interface of observations and models, a theme I established during my PhD where I used a data assimilation framework to combine satellite observations of the terrestrial biosphere with an ecosystem model (DALEC). Since then, the direction of my research has shifted towards the use of data from manipulation experiments (e.g. Free-Air CO2 Enrichment experiments and drought treatments) and eddy covariance (FLUXNET). I am particularly interested in integrating novel data-driven insights into international climate models, thus ensuring research impact that is directly policy-relevant.

To understand how plants will respond to global change, I use a range of models of varying complexity, from simple (GDAY), to the more complex: stand (MAESPA), land surface (CABLE), dynamic vegetation (LPJ-GUESS; SDGVM) and coupled-climate (ACCESS) models.

I am an Associate Editor of Biogeosciences and a member of the Editorial Advisory Board for Global Change Biology and New Phytologist. I am the co-chair of the leadership committee for the CABLE land surface model. I am also a member of the Scientific Steering Committee for the AmazonFACE experiment.

Feel free to drop me an email if any of that sounds interesting! In particular, potential PhD students that have an interest in exploring research questions related to the carbon and water cycles and their responses to global change.
